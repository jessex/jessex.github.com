For the past week or so, I have been checking out Quora. I had been aware of Quora for at least a couple of months, at least as far back as when steam began to pick up near the end of 2010. And during this time, I kept telling myself, "Yeah I'll definitely sign up soon and give it a look." And due to some combination of malaise and unknown forces, it just never came to fruition until recently. The question was: why have I not yet signed up for Quora? I saw it everywhere: Hacker News, Techcrunch, Mashable, in tweets, in blog postings, everywhere. Maybe I was feeling inertia because I tend to shy away from things which build seemingly exponential curves of hype, for some reason. I guess I was ultimately concerned that I would try it out and would be horribly disappointed by some as of yet unknown flaw, further rationalizing the aforementioned shyness.

Haha, whoops!

After playing with Quora for a mere week, my only remaining question is: why do I enjoy it so much?

Quora is a Q&A smorgasbord which seeks to become the go-to site for anything and everything that may need an answer: "the best possible resource for someone who wants to know about the question" (from their About page). As is a rising trend nowadays, Quora is powered by crowd intelligence and knowledge: users ask questions which other users then answer and/or comment on. Questions are organized into different topics, which can also be created by users. Because of this, topics are extremely broad spectrum and you can find questions on everything from Least Angle Regression to quotes from The Office to the differences between Communism and Socialism.

But this is an elevator pitch similar to that which you would hear for a plethora of other sites. So what is that it-factor for me? Could it be the feature set? The multitude of contributions that can be made? Well, content is organized into topics and subtopics, which are creatable and editable by users. Questions in turn are grouped into topics, with a single question having one to many topic classifications. Questions are asked by users and can be edited by other users, be it for wording or for topic organization. Users can answer questions or comment on the questions themselves: two distinct actions with their own purposes and contributions. Answers can of course be edited or deleted by the original author.

Feedback to answers is particularly multifaceted. Of course, there is the standard upvote/downvote. If you feel an answer is appropriate and particularly high quality: upvote. If it is very low quality or superficial: downvote. But what if the answer is not poor in quality? Maybe it is just a bit inappropriate or incorrect in some manner but you don't think it warrants something as harsh and foreboding as a downvote? Mark it as "Not Helpful." And one can do the inverse: "Thank" the user for their answer. Additionally, you can leave a comment on an answer and even suggest an edit to be made.

It all makes for a diverse experience, for sure. But other collective knowledge and Q&A sites offer similarly rich feature sets and I do not harbor little titillating, giggly feelings for many of them (see: Yahoo Answers). Is it simply the concept of contributing to a knowledge base which intrigues me? While that definitely is an intriguing concept for a philosopher such as myself (philosopher in this case meaning "lover of knowledge" from the Greek philosophia), that does not explain the amorosity for Quora: I adore Wikipedia but I hardly ever contribute, even though there is both opportunity and motive.

Yesterday, I read a blog posting by Reg Braithwaite about why he was leaving Quora. The summary of his reasoning is that while Quora has a community, it is not a community in and of itself. As he puts it:

You have to buy into the larger, nobler goal of building a repository of answers to questions first, and then the community comes as a bonus, second. Whereas a place like Hacker News feels like it's a community first, and any accumulation of knowledge that might accrete is secondary to the community feeling.

That second sentence is precisely why I do enjoy HN: the sense of community and camaraderie that springs up in the interactions between users. But I read and re-read that first sentence a handful of times and it struck me: that's exactly why I enjoy Quora! Quora is not about community for community's sake; rather, Quora's community is structured for and aimed toward a very clear, benevolent goal. Maybe it is that dash of Socialist that resides somewhere inside of me, but the idea of a community which has come together for a purpose is something which I find exciting. The purpose is above the community and individual users are contributors to something big and beautiful.

Surely, many Quora users are not there for a higher goal. Perhaps many of them are there for the reasons that Braithwaite outlined as for why he was not. Perhaps, they want to make friends and interact with other people, and Quora just happens to provide a setting which allows them to do so. But the communal structure is set up to build the ultimate knowledge base, first and foremost. Anything else comes second.

The public profiles, the ability to personalize biographical text to individual topics, the Wiki-style logging and revision history, the changeability of all public space, the commenting and feedback systems: these are all aimed, at least in part, toward compelling community members to increase the overall quality of the content that they and others are contributing to Quora.

The ability to follow topics, questions and people, the recommendation system, the activity and notification feeds, the distinctions between Open, Best and Unorganized Questions: these are all designed to help draw you to the information and content that you are most likely to interact with and contribute to.

Quora has pretty brilliantly structured itself so that users will keep coming back. Contributing, organizing, improving. Asking and answering. Giving and receiving feedback. Creating and editing.

Braithwaite (Raganwald) decided to leave Quora because he wanted to focus on communities, as in community-first communities. This is perfectly reasonable: hell, I contribute to Hacker News for precisely those same reasons. That's why it is by far my most visited website. But the pendulum is swinging: Quora has established the kind of place that I really want to be a part of. I've spent far too much time there recently, and it does not seem likely to recede anytime soon.

A community with a virtuous purpose: what a novel idea.
